# Lead Safe Lansing Project (2026)
### Summary:
This project examines the effectiveness of Lead Safe Lansing’s (LSL) in targeting ZIP codes with high percentages of children under 6 with elevated blood lead levels (EBLL), which are moderately associated with poverty. While this initiative identified and addressed some of the highest-risk areas, average blood lead levels remain higher than in neighborhoods less focused by the intervention. Also, the number of projects declined from 2010 to 2022, which might have reflected inflation. The paper will also examine the relationship between project count and inflation, as measured by the Producer Price Index (PPI), and find plausible evidence of an association after filtering out outliers.

--- 
### Main Findings:

1. The moderate poverty-EBLL relationship indicates that LSL is typically reaching the right areas; the only ZIP code without project coverage has a relatively low poverty rate and EBLL percentage, suggesting that resources are not being wasted in low-risk areas. But the line plot gives a more nuanced picture. Although EBLL declines over time across all five quintile groups, Q4 remains persistently elevated throughout the period, whereas Q5, the highest project-concentration group, shows a steep decline. This is consistent with a dose-response relationship, in which significant EBLL reductions occur only at the highest levels of program intensity. This indicates moderate investment, such as in Q4, may not be enough to produce a change in health status, and that LSL’s resources at present leave meaningful resource gaps in mid-concentration areas.
   
2. Contrary to my expectations, higher construction inflation is associated with lower mean rehabilitation costs per project, not higher. This conflicting effect is most likely to be explained by the budget-constrained scoping effect: when prices of material increase, LSL will scope down to smaller projects with lower construction costs rather than reducing the number of projects altogether and, thus, compressing average rehabilitation project costs at each time they are rehabilitated under cost-induced inflation pressure. The lack of a substantial inflation-to-project-count relationship can also be explained by non-inflation factors, such as why we observe low project count from 2010 to 2012, the timing of the grant cycle, and minimal state funding, not material cost, contributing to the declining number of projects, and the 2020–2022 decrease is confounded by COVID-19, which limits in-person abatement work. In response to RQ2, rehabilitation costs are negatively associated with changes in PPI inflation over time, which is not necessarily due to declining input prices but rather reflects LSL strategically limiting project size due to cost constraints. At the same time, the data may be too skewed to be meaningful for statistical testing, which makes them slightly less credible.
